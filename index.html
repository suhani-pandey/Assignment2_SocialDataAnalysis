The Crossover

Day and night.
Two cities.

Let's start with a simple question: When do crimes happen in San Francisco?

On weekdays, crime follows the work schedule. It starts low at night, rises in the morning, and peaks at 6PM — right when people leave work.

Why 6PM? More people on streets means more opportunities. Commuters carry valuables. Parking lots fill up. Thieves know this.

But weekends are different. Look at the orange line. Crime stays lower all day, then suddenly spikes at night.

At 10PM, something dramatic happens. The weekend line crosses above the weekday line. This is "the crossover."

Why? Friday and Saturday nights = bars, clubs, parties. More people stay out until 2-3AM. More alcohol, more crowds, fewer witnesses.3

Weekend nights see +23% more crime per hour than weekday nights. The city literally transforms after 10PM on Fridays.

Key insight: Crime doesn't just follow the clock. It follows human behavior. Where people go, crime follows.

The Hinge

The week bends
on Friday night.

Now let's zoom out. This heatmap shows every day of the week and every hour of the day. Brighter colors = more crime.

Notice the pattern? Monday through Thursday look similar. Crime rises from 8AM, peaks around noon-6PM, then drops at night.

Why this pattern? People wake up, go to work, run errands, go home. More people out = more crime opportunities. Simple as that.

But Friday and Saturday? Look at how they burn bright orange late into the night. This is the "hinge" — where the week transforms.

What happens Friday night? San Francisco's entertainment zones come alive. Bars, restaurants, clubs stay packed until 2AM.4

More people + alcohol + late hours = perfect storm. Fights break out. Drunk people become targets. Cars get broken into while owners party.

One important note: Empty doesn't mean safe. Tuesday 4AM is dark on this map because no one's around to report crimes, not because nothing bad happens.

Bottom line: If you're planning to be out late in San Francisco, Friday and Saturday nights carry the highest risk. The data is crystal clear on this.

Seven Clocks

Seven crimes.
Seven different rhythms.

Not all crimes happen at the same time. Each type has its own temporal pattern. Let's explore why.

ROBBERY peaks at 2AM Saturday mornings. Intoxicated bar-goers make easier targets, dark streets provide cover, and fewer witnesses are present — a perfect convergence for street robbery.56

WEAPON OFFENSES spike late weekend nights in entertainment zones.4 Alcohol escalates conflicts to violence, and nightlife concentration creates geographic hotspots.

DRUG OFFENSES peak at 2PM weekdays. This doesn't show when drugs are used — it shows when police patrol.8

Daytime shifts mean officers conduct more street enforcement in drug market areas. More police presence equals more arrests. This is an enforcement peak, not a consumption peak.

WARRANTS peak at 4-5PM weekdays. Officers serve warrants during business hours at shift end. This measures administrative scheduling, not criminal behavior.

VEHICLE THEFT peaks at 6-10PM. Cars parked for dinner or overnight sit unguarded for extended periods.7 Thieves exploit these predictable patterns.

MISSING PERSON reports peak at noon. This shows when absence is noticed (failed work arrivals, missed appointments), not when people actually go missing.

NON-CRIMINAL calls (noise, welfare checks) follow business hours. People report issues during the day; at night, disturbances go unreported. Reporting behavior shapes the data.

Key insight: Some crimes follow opportunity (robbery, theft). Others follow police schedules (drugs, warrants). This distinction matters for interpretation.

What This Means

The city doesn't
just change at night.
It transforms by the hour.

So what does all this mean? After analyzing 22 years and 1.2 million crimes, here's the truth: Crime follows people.

When people commute to work, crime peaks at 6PM. When people party on Friday nights, robbery spikes at 2AM. When police patrol at lunchtime, drug arrests go up. It's not random — it's predictable.

But here's the catch: This data shows reported crimes, not all crimes. A quiet Tuesday morning might just mean no one's around to call 911, not that nothing bad is happening. Drug arrests at 2PM show police activity, not when people use drugs most.

What can you do with this? If you live in SF, avoid walking alone late Friday/Saturday nights. If you're a policymaker, maybe put more officers in entertainment zones on weekends. If you park downtown, do it before 6PM on weekdays.

The 9AM city and the 2AM city are completely different places. Same streets, different risks. Now you know when the clock strikes danger.

Data & Methodology

Incident data spans 2003–2025 from SFPD official records, standardized and cleaned. All averages are per-day means within each day-type (weekday or weekend). Crime categories are standardized across reporting periods. The dataset records reported incidents — not all crimes, and not all harms.
