Chapter 01

The city breathes.
Crime breathes with it.

Most crime coverage treats safety like a static number — a temperature that rises and falls over months or years. But that misses the real story. San Francisco doesn't have a crime problem. It has several overlapping problems, each running on its own schedule.

When you map 22 years of police incidents onto the clock, the city's daily choreography becomes visible. Morning brings its own rhythm. The evening commute another. And after midnight — especially on weekends — a different city appears entirely.

"Midnight isn't just later.
It's another city."

Chapter 03

One chart.
Two cities.

Compare a typical weekday to a typical weekend day, and a story emerges in two acts. During the day, weekdays dominate — the city is at work, and so is crime. But watch what happens after 10PM.

The weekend line surges past the weekday line and doesn't come back down until after 4AM. This isn't just a statistical quirk. It reflects the social geography of San Francisco: bars, clubs, nightlife — and the opportunities they create.

Chapter 04

The week has
a hinge.

It bends on Friday night and doesn't straighten out until Sunday morning. The heatmap reveals that the weekend surge isn't evenly distributed — it concentrates into one intense 36-hour window.

Monday through Thursday? Nearly identical. Then Friday arrives, and the city's nighttime profile shifts dramatically. The darkest cells in this entire dataset form a diagonal band: Friday 10PM → Saturday 3AM.

The implication is precise: it's not just "night" that matters. It's late-week night. That distinction changes everything about how you'd think about prevention, patrol, or policy.

Takeaway

The city doesn't
just get dark.
It transforms.

Twenty-two years of data tells a story that a single headline never could. San Francisco's crime pattern isn't a wave that rises and falls — it's a living, shifting thing that responds to the day of the week, the hour, and the social geography of neighborhoods.

The daytime city and the 2AM city share an address, but not much else. Different crimes. Different rhythms. Different stories.

Which means any serious response to crime in San Francisco has to grapple with that complexity — not just ask "is it getting better?" but when, where, and which kind?

Data & Methodology

Incident data spans 2003–2025 from SFPD official records, standardized and cleaned. All averages are per-day means within each day-type (weekday or weekend). Crime categories are standardized across reporting periods.
